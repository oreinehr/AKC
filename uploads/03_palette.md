# Color Palette — 3 Bets

> **Principle, locked from positioning.** *One accent, two intensities. The accent IS the register switch.*
> Quiet intensity for boardroom / proposals / print. Loud intensity for feed / digital / motion.
> The neutrals (black + off-white) hold steady across all surfaces; the accent flexes to match the room.
>
> Each bet below picks a different accent. Pick the accent; the system does the rest.

---

## Why one accent (and not three)

Multi-color systems are how teams ship. Single-accent systems are how *people* are remembered. Felipe Pantone runs gradients because he's a movement; senior individual brands (Bierut, Hawtin's Plastikman, Tobias Van Schneider) almost always commit to one signature color and let discipline do the differentiation work. Memorable beats expressive when the brand is one person.

Discipline is also the bet. Calibrated by craft → demonstrate calibration *with* the visual system, not just *in* it.

## Bet A — Signal Red

**Thesis.** Black + a precise red is the most disciplined option on the table. It says *I know exactly what one note I need.* This is the techno-purist bet: minimum surface, maximum signal. Red carries the kinetic charge of every artist on your hero list (Pantone, Maller, Hawtin's Plastikman) without losing the editorial seriousness of Bierut.

## Bet C — Court & Phosphor

**Thesis.** The wild card — and the most differentiated. Phosphor / chartreuse is the color of generative-kinetic art (Felipe Pantone), tennis-ball saturation pushed electric, and rave-LED energy. Quiet intensity is a deep court-grass green; loud intensity is full electric chartreuse. This bet says *I am the senior CD who is also alive in 2026 culture.* It is the loudest bet on memorability and the riskiest for conservative buyers.

### System

| Role | Hex | RGB | HSL | CMYK | Pantone (closest) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Foundation Black | `#0A0A0A` | 10, 10, 10 | 0°, 0%, 4% | 0 / 0 / 0 / 96 | Black 6 C |
| Foundation Off-White | `#F4F1EA` | 244, 241, 234 | 42°, 26%, 94% | 0 / 1 / 4 / 4 | Warm Gray 1 C |
| **Accent — Quiet** (court green) | `#1F4029` | 31, 64, 41 | 138°, 35%, 19% | 52 / 0 / 36 / 75 | 627 C |
| **Accent — Loud** (phosphor) | `#D8FF3D` | 216, 255, 61 | 70°, 100%, 62% | 15 / 0 / 76 / 0 | 389 C |

### Where the two intensities deploy

- **Quiet (court green)** — text emphasis, body trim, card edges, letterhead, footer rules. Reads as a sober deep green, not chartreuse.
- **Loud (phosphor)** — fill blocks, motion accents, social tile blocks (with black type laid on top), highlight stripes, video lower-third fills.

### Critical usage rule

**Phosphor is a fill, not text.** Loud phosphor fails contrast as text on white (~1.7:1) and on black (~14:1 — ironically passes there as text on dark, but reads neon).

Pattern: phosphor as a **block of color with black type sitting on it** = ~14:1 contrast = AAA. This is how Felipe Pantone, &Walsh, and most contemporary kinetic brands deploy it. Use this rule strictly.

### Accessibility (WCAG)

- Black on Off-White: **17.5 : 1** — AAA
- Quiet Court Green on Off-White: **9 : 1** — AAA
- Black on Phosphor (block): **14 : 1** — AAA (the canonical use)
- Phosphor as text: **fails** — never use phosphor for type on light backgrounds.

### Locks in / locks out

- *Locks in:* Differentiation. Memorability. Modern/2026-fluent. Tennis thread is read directly. Generative-art crowd reads it instantly.
- *Locks out:* SAP-tier conservative finance. May read "design agency" instead of "creative director" to certain enterprise audiences. Requires disciplined execution — phosphor used sloppily reads junior.

### The lume rule

A watch dial has one signature color and a *lume* — a separate luminous compound that only activates in specific conditions (low light, motion). Phosphor is to this brand what lume is to a watch: present, structural, **but only visible in defined contexts.**

**Phosphor appears in (~1 of 10 surfaces):**

- Motion / kinetic work showcases — video accents, generative samples, moving-image tiles.
- Time-stamped or year-stamped graphics — "2026" campaign tags, newsletter issue numbers.
- AI-content or technology-forward tags — signals current/forward without putting "AI" in copy.
- Single hero highlights on a landing-page section (not all of them).
- Generative or kinetic design samples.

**Phosphor never appears in:**

- Stationery (letterhead, business cards) — primary cobalt only.
- Email signature — primary only.
- Print proposals, RFPs, decks — primary only.
- As text — phosphor fails contrast as type. **Always a fill with black type on top.**
- The logo — never recolor the AKC mark phosphor.

**Frequency rule.** If phosphor is everywhere, it becomes wallpaper. If it's never used, why have it. Aim for ~1 of every 10 surfaces — roughly once per project category.

### Gradient directions — LOCKED (2026-04-29, alongside typography Bet B)

Four candidates reduced to two locked + two situational. Pairings driven by Bet B's kinetic Migra display.

**Locked — use freely in loud register:**

1. **Cobalt → Phosphor** — primary kinetic gradient. Pairs with Migra display moments. The brand's signature kinetic mark.
2. **Black → Cobalt** — premium / cinematic depth. Dark-mode hero blocks, video lower-third backgrounds.

**Situational — use deliberately:**

3. **Navy → Cobalt** — tonal cobalt. Backgrounds, photography overlays, deep-color washes.
4. **Black → Phosphor** — rare drama. Reserved for the most dramatic kinetic moments. Counts toward phosphor's 1-of-10 lume rule.

See `04_typography.md` for full reasoning behind the lock.

### Open decisions before stationery

- **Logo color.** Black always (most disciplined — recommended), or cobalt in loud-register contexts (feed, video)? White on dark backgrounds is already supplied.
- **Print finishes.** Foil-stamp cobalt on bone, letterpress black on bone, or both (foil for business cards, letterpress for letterhead)?

## Reusable template — what transfers to client work

Patterns to lift for the next personal-brand engagement:

1. **One-accent, two-intensities** as the structural rule. Keeps the brand disciplined and gives content teams a built-in register switch.
2. **Three distinct bets, not three flavors.** Each accent must close a door, not just hold one open.
3. **Accessibility ratios in the palette doc itself.** Print and digital teams stop guessing.
4. **Pantone closest-match called out.** When the brand goes to print procurement, the conversion is already done.
5. **Locks in / locks out** for each option. Forces honest conversation about who the palette excludes.
6. **Primary + exception (the lume rule).** A primary system holds ~90% of surfaces; one exception accent earns the remaining ~10% with explicit rules. Prevents palette dilution while keeping room for personality.
